Darkness. Darkness; all-enveloping, all-being. Darkness and darkness only.

A crisp click — a spotlight flickers alight, tearing a circular gap into the black fabric of a stage. The surrounding nil yields to the blinding white void, partially, lurking around the light's radius, waiting to snap back into place.


Suspended above this white abyss stands a simple wooden frame, cradling a canvas, blank: as perfect as it ever will be.

Dust lingers heavy in the air. Specks of it drift about the frame's spindly legs, shining in the light like lifeless fireflies.


The dust suddenly swirls as a boy steps into the glow. His features are youthful, inexperienced, and his frame, clothed in a thin black sweater, rivals the stand's in seeming fragility. In thin fingers, he holds a large brush and a pallette, both new and unstained.

He stops beside the stand, giving it a reluctant examinatory glance. Without warning, his gaze jumps abruptly from the white canvas to the surrounding nothingness. At first, only blankness stares back, but as the boy squints, the tones and shades of different objects begin to resolve themselves in the dark. His eyes make out the edges of the stage some small distance away and beyond, black slender figures mingle politely in a small crowd. White smiling masks hide their faces as they observe objects unseen in the distance, conversing in hushed tones that blend into a low hum.

The boy's heartbeat quickens. Of the figures, one or two are looking at him.


He turns to the blank painting, drawing in a deep breath. One has to start somewhere. He picks a colour, and after one last moment of hesitation, guides the brush across the canvas in a bold stroke of blue.

Then yellow — just a splatter. A dark purple follows, with a shy dab of red.


He looks over his shoulder. Two more of the masked figures observe him now. Is he doing it right?

Slowly, shapes begin to form on the canvas, squirming like worms as they are reshaped and redefined with every stroke. The boy's movements grow faster, more brash, finding patterns in the way the paint spreads.

He smiles. For a long time, this image had sat in the corner of his mind; of his soul; and now it unravels right in front of his eyes.


Some of the shapes begin to drip like melting snowmen, but a touch of his brush repairs any inconsistencies. The colours begin to bleed into each other, but another coat of vibrant colour hides the mishaps.

He stops abruptly, as if the canvas has suddenly disappeared. And it has, for now it hides under a blanket of paint. Completion.

With a smile, he examines his work. The contrast is a bit off and the colours are slightly wrong. Nothing too bad.


He tears his eyes away from his masterpiece to face the crowd. It stands silent, observing. A couple of the figures murmur something to each other.

The boy waits. Nothing happens. Is he missing something?

With a sudden realization, he returns to his painting and scrawls "My first painting (judge plez)" in large, blood-red letters across the top.


But the crowd remains undisturbed. The boy waits some more.

He must be missing something. But the picture, it's...

With a frantic eye, he scans his work, again, this time finding even more errors. They are a few, but the longer he looks, the more of them spring up from the paint like colourful weeds. Is it that bad?


He jolts as a polished shoe steps atop the stage with a sharp tap. One of the figures. It moves in slow, long strides, stopping beside the boy and his mess of colour, tilting its mask with a tint of curiosity.

For a long moment, the silence is broken only by the hammering of the boy's pulse. A volatile mixture of dread and anticipation fills his lungs. Then the figure speaks.


"For a first piece, this is...



"exemplary".


The boy's heart skips a beat. The figure continues.

"Nevertheless, countless flaws are present. The contrast is lacking in places. The red is oversaturated, and many lines lack cohesion, which...."

It drones on, but the boy isn't listening — his smile withers. Of course.


He looks back at his chaos of colours with brewing disgust. The errors and imperfections stare him down. Screeching laughter — their screeching laughter — fills the stage, echoing into the darkness. He covers his ears, but the noise persists, hammering into his skull.


Why? He did well! He should be happy.

Is he not? Why is he not? What was he expecting? Surely not more than this.

This makes no sense. Is there something wrong with him?


His legs shudder, then give way as he crumbles to his knees.


What is wrong with him? Why? He should be happy. Happy. Happy. Stop. Happy.


With a yell, the boy jolts then rises and rushes towards the stand, his hands outstretched. Pale fingers reach for the canvas, he wants it gone, gone!
