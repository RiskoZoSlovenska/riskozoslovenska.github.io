Window

In my room, I have a window. It’s not big, but that doesn’t matter. I like it.

My room is lovely. Bright blue walls, warm wooden floor, white door that rests open. The window sits to the side, a grand decoration amongst all the other curiosities that adorn the walls.

I look out the window sometimes, just a passing glance as I go about. Other times, I bring out a chair and stare at the other side when I have nothing else to do.

It’s a strange window. Plastic frame, rectangular shape — nothing unusual at a first glance. What it shows, however… that’s what makes it extraordinary.


The other side is wonderful. Lush green grass and spotless blue skies, stretching out as far as the eye can see. Lulling birdsong hangs in the air, in tune with the warm breeze. It changes all the time, too. One day, it’s a beautiful meadow, the other, a charming misty forest, and yet another, a shimmering sunset on a golden beach. Each place is more breathtaking than the last, and the window always has a new scene to show.

I’ve seen wondrous, amazing things through the window. It tells me stories, and I watch with rapt attention. Tales of brave adventurers marching through unforgiving mountains. Recollections of epic battles fought on the bloodiest battlefields. Heartbreaking fables of heroes past and of their sacrifices — the sweet ring of victory, the bitter grasp of death, I’ve seen it all through the window.

It’s not always stories, however. Sometimes the window shows me symbols, letters, numbers. They assemble into words, phrases, sentences. I try to read them all, but there’s too many to possibly see every single one. I still try.

The outside writes of different people, all amazing and unique. They share thoughts, opinions, feelings, all hoping that someone sees them — that someone cares. I write my own on a piece of paper and press it up against the window; I hope that someone sees them — that someone cares.

Day by day, I sit at the window, longer every time. The thrill draws me nearer, makes me wish I was on the other side. The room becomes blurred and dull, yet the window keeps shining.

The other side is mesmerizing, and I let the window enthrall me with its magic. More and more of my senses surrender to the scenery outside, returning to the room only momentarily. I keep watching, keep writing, keep hoping.


Day by day, I sit at the window, and it is beginning to be hard to look away. The walls of my room have gone blank; the door has closed and disappeared as well. Only the window is left now.

The window is as exciting and vibrant as always. I continue gazing — adventures play in front of my eyes, letters and words and sentences fly by. I do not remember anything other than the window now, and I’m not even sure there’s anyone on the other side. I write my words, I prop papers up against the warm glass, and I hope. I hope that perhaps they are not ignored, that perhaps someone would actually notice if I stopped. Unlikely, but… Perhaps.

And so I write this from the small blank cave I used to call my room. The other side still shines through the window, and I’m starting to forget who I am or who I want to be. The window is all-consuming, and I’ve slipped past the point of no return. It doesn’t matter, I suppose. Does anything? The walls keep closing in, and I have little space to move now. My back hurts, my eyes are locked forward. I no longer know who I am or who I want to be.
