“…and thus, I declare you banished to the Frostbitten Peak indefinitely.”

Berum had been waiting for those words. He knew they were coming – knew of their inevitability – but no matter how much he had tried to brace for them, they still rang cold and sharp, like a blade slicing the air. He would have shivered if he hadn’t been doing so since morning.

He shifted just slightly, trying to relieve the pressure around his wrists. The tightly-wound ropes around his hands, knots soldered by flame, only dug deeper into his skin as he fought them. They did not yield, however, and he gave up shortly. The frigid wind that blew in every corner of Altum did nothing to make him more comfortable, either. Berum gritted his teeth harder, trying to stop them from chattering.


Word about the twisted murdered had gotten out relatively quickly, and the official banishment had attracted quite a gathering. Spirits, Michi and Moki alike flocked around the small wooden podium upon which Berum was seated, murmuring amongst each other quietly. Berum kept his head down, ears drooping; he had no desire for visual contact.


The Altumi elder standing beside Berum scanned the crowd wearily. His eyes jumped from face to face, finally coming to rest upon a tall spirit in the back.

“Ah, Suva. Would you mind being the escort?” the elder said politely, but with an undertone suggesting that it wasn’t a question. The crowd fractured into two, allowing the summoned spirit to come forth. He stopped about a tail’s length away from the kneeling Friolian, silent, but with a menacing air about him.

Berum looked up to meet his gaze. Suva – as the elder addressed him – was relatively young, barely much older than Berum himself. He was tall and muscular, yet not as broad as one would expect. Unlike the majority of spirits in the surrounding crowd, he wore no vest, nor hat, nor scarf – not even a pair of warm gloves. His eyes shone cold as he stared the smaller spirit down.

“Gladly.” He replied slowly, steel thick in his voice. Berum looked – was forced to look, rather – down again under Suva’s icy glare.


“Good.” The elder said, handing Suva a battered bag. Suva took it and then bent down to grab Berum firmly by the arm. Berum flinched, enough to make Suva pause for a second.

Names, places, faces. There were surprisingly more locations than people, however. A hut, one of the mountainous peaks somewhere in Altum, a frozen lake nearby, a small hill with a glorious view of the sunrise and dozens upon dozens of other places, most of them seemingly tied to the extremes of the harsh environment.

Berum groaned as the larger spirit hoisted him to his feet; his muscles were sore, he was cold and Suva’s grip was firm, almost painful. The two walked in silence, no one from the crowd daring to follow.


The town Berum had been brought to sat atop a plateau, with its edge matching the landform’s. Suva stopped at the edge of the precipice, and letting go of Berum, gave the shivering spirit a quick look. Dissatisfied, he opened the bag and rummaged through it, finally withdrawing a black cloak. Begrudgingly, he draped it over Berum. It was relatively small and rough on the inside, yet surprisingly warm. The smaller spirit gave Suva a grateful, quizzical look but said nothing.


No longer shivering violently, he took a small step forward and peered into the swirling white abyss below. It was impossible to see how far down the bottom was, and the cliff face was smooth ice. “How are we getting down?” he asked.

“We’re going to fly.” Suva sneered, with a hit of amusement at Berum’s reaction. He pulled a length of rope out of the bag, taking a step towards Berum.

“But-” the smaller was interrupted mid-sentence as the larger grabbed him again and hoisted him up so that their backs were touching. He then proceeded to tie the rope around them, at the waist and below the armpits. With the bag slung around his chest, he turned to the open air.

“Are- Are you sure you can hold both of us?” Berum squeaked, not trying to hide the anxiety in his voice. But it was too late – Suva was already running towards the edge, arms outstretched. The ground suddenly vanished below their feet, allowing the duo to plummet down into the white void. The uncomfortable sensation of freefall rippled through Berum, but it lasted only briefly. Suva’s borrowed wings caught air quickly and they were off.

They landed to rest several hours later. Suva, while his flying was nothing short of impressive, looked weary. It was impossible to see the sun through the thick, snow-saturated winds, but the sky was definitely darkening as well.

They made camp in a small cave, which Berum found surprisingly familiar. Suva got a small fire going, and the two had a meagre dinner of dried meat. They sat in silence for a while.


“Why not kill me?” Suva looked up, a curious expression on his face. Berum continued. “I mean, I’ve done nothing but murder. Why not do the same? Aren’t you afraid I’ll survive and continue?”

There was a short silence before the Altumi answered. “Because we aren’t like you.” Berum recoiled; the words felt like a stab in the chest. “We don’t believe in killing our own kin, no matter what they’ve done. Still… you could argue that some deserve it.” He gave him a cold glare, and Berum couldn’t help but look down. “And either way, who says you’ll survive anyways? Frostbitten Peak is known for being the one place where the storms never stop. Perhaps during the night, if you’re lucky.”

Pause.

“The elders must trust you to send you alone with me.” Berum said.

Suva was silent a while before he sighed. “I have a… reputation, you could say. Not many spirits know their way around this corner of Altum too well. Especially around the Peak. And I’m not the type of person to mess around with something serious.”


Berum has no reply to that, and the conversation died as suddenly as it began. He leaned against the ice-cold wall and closed his eyes, replaying thoughts, memories, feelings in his head. Never before had he felt so conflicted. Eventually, the memories transformed and warped into dreams, and he lapsed into a light sleep.


Suva gave his escortee a thoughtful look before deciding it wouldn’t hurt to get some rest as well; this wasn’t a place where the Dark Wolves would come often. He put out the fire, checked Berum’s bindings and then laid down, drifting off into sleep himself.

Suva awoke with a start, blinking in surprise at the sun shining into the small cave. He had been expecting to be woken by Berum during an attempt to escape, but it appeared that attempt was never made. The Friolian sat in the same spot as he had been in last night, wide awake and seemingly mesmerized by the sunrise. His ears twitched as Suva shifted to sit up, but he said nothing.

The storm had cleared up during the night, allowing a spectacular view of the early morning sun. They watched it rise high above the horizon together, marvelling at its golden beauty until it was too bright to look at.


A steep climb up a small mountain followed. Even with Berum once again tied to Suva’s back, the Altumi spirit seemed to flow up the terrain. Once at the top, it was simply a matter of another quick dash until they were flying again.

The sky was clear and brisk at first, with no clouds to decorate the blue canvas. Yet as they flew on, the weather got more and more aggressive, as if they were approaching something that didn’t wish to be approached. Gusts of snow and hail screamed around them once more, and for a second, Berum wondered whether the ropes holding him would burst up. Thankfully, they didn’t, and soon the two spirits were back on the ground.

“We walk from here.” Berum didn’t question it and trailed after Suva mutely. Despite the thick cloak draped around him, the furious wind howling around them bit into his flesh, clawing at it, sapping his warmth away. He shivered.


Another hour or so – Berum wasn’t sure – passed before they stopped. This time, it was with an air of finality; a silent “we’re here,” passed between the two spirits. Suva rummaged through the bag again and retrieved a knife. It was Berum’s knife.

Wasting no time, he walked up to Berum, slid the fine blade in-between his paws and with a firm, sudden motion, the rope fell to the ground. He did the same for the bindings around his feet, finishing by giving Berum a stiff shove. The Friolian tumbled into the deep snow, wincing as small drifts of snow blew up under and into his cloak. Wide-eyed and shaking, he met the gaze of the larger – flashing steel in both Suva’s hand and eyes. Suva stood there for few tense moments, motionless, flexing his grip on the blade’s handle. Then the tension snapped; he sighed, discarded both weapon and bag at Berum’s feet and turned away.

Berum grabbed the two items without hesitation. Carefully, be brushed the knife clean and was about to put it back into the bag when a small, wooden box inside caught his eye. His collection.

“Wait, why did you le-” he started, looking up. But Suva was gone, and besides the howling, icy winds, Berum was alone.
